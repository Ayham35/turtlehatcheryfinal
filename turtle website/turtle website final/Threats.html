Threats To Turtles

The Main Threats to turtles

Poaching


Turtle eggs and adult turtles are illegally harvested and traded for their meat, shells, and other body parts. These turtles and their products are often sold on the black market, both domestically and internationally.

Habitat Destruction

Urbanization, tourism development, and coastal infrastructure projects often result in the destruction and degradation of nesting beaches, foraging areas, and other critical habitats for turtles. These activities lead to the loss of nesting sites and disrupt the natural ecosystem.

Pollution

Pollution, including plastic waste and marine debris, poses a significant threat to turtles in Sri Lanka. Turtles can ingest or become entangled in plastic, which can cause injuries, infections, and even death. Pollution from industrial activities, agricultural runoff, and improper waste disposal can also contaminate their habitats.


How can we prevent the threats?

By combining numerous approaches, Sri Lanka aims to protect and conserve its turtle populations and their habitats, ensuring their long-term survival in the face of ongoing threats especially from the ones mentioned above that include poaching, pollution, habitat destruction etc.

Some of those initiatives taken up by govermment include the following:

  1. Protected Areas and Conservation Projects: Sri Lanka has established several protected areas and marine sanctuaries to safeguard turtle nesting beaches and critical habitats. These areas are managed and monitored by wildlife authorities and conservation organizations to ensure the conservation of turtle populations.
  2. Nesting Beach Monitoring and Protection: Monitoring programs are in place to track nesting activities and protect turtle nests from poaching and disturbance. Local communities, along with conservation organizations, participate in beach patrols to identify and relocate nests to safer areas if needed.
  3. Regulation and Law Enforcement: Sri Lanka has legislation in place to protect turtles, including the Fauna and Flora Protection Ordinance. This legislation prohibits the hunting, killing, or capture of turtles and their eggs.
  4. Sustainable Fishing Practices: Efforts are made to promote sustainable fishing practices that minimize bycatch and turtle mortality. Fishermen are encouraged to use turtle excluder devices (TEDs) in their nets, which allow turtles to escape if accidentally caught.
  5. Coastal Zone Management: Sri Lanka emphasizes integrated coastal zone management to balance economic development with environmental conservation.
